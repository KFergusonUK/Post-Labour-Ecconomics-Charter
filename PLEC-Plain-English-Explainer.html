The Post-Labour
Economic Charter

A plain English guide to how we ensure everyone benefits from automation — not just shareholders.

Robots and AI are getting better at doing jobs. That's mostly a good thing — it makes things cheaper to produce and frees people from dull, repetitive work.


But there's a problem. The money saved by replacing workers doesn't automatically go back to the people who lost their jobs. Without action, wealth concentrates at the top while everyone else struggles.


The Post-Labour Economic Charter (PLEC) fixes this — by ensuring that when a company profits from automation, a fair share comes back to society.

Not a tax on success. A dividend on shared prosperity — because the knowledge, infrastructure, and society that made automation possible belongs to all of us.

The Problem

A company replaces 1,000 workers with AI. Revenue stays the same. Costs fall dramatically. Profits soar.

Those 1,000 people need to pay rent, buy food, raise families. But their income is gone. And the company that replaced them pays nothing toward the cost of that displacement.

Multiply that across every sector, every country, over twenty years — and you have a crisis.

The Solution

PLEC requires companies whose workforce falls below a sector-appropriate threshold to contribute a share of their revenue to a public fund.

That fund pays a Universal Basic Income to every adult — enough to live with dignity, whether or not they have a job.

As automation grows, contributions grow. As costs fall, your money goes further. The system adjusts automatically.

How It Works

1

Measure

Each company's human wage bill is measured as a percentage of total revenue, compared to its sector's threshold.

2

Calculate

If below the threshold, the company pays an Automation Dividend Contribution — a percentage of the gap.

3

Pool

Contributions flow into a National Automation Dividend Fund — a sovereign public trust, independently managed.

4

Distribute

The fund pays a Universal Basic Income to every adult, replacing the existing benefits system over time.

Key Features

⚖

Sector-fair thresholds

Healthcare and education have higher thresholds than retail and agriculture — sectors that can't easily automate aren't penalised.

🛡

Anti-avoidance built in

Offshore labour counts at only 0.33 weighting. Shell companies and fragmented subsidiaries are caught by connected enterprise rules.

🌱

Startup protection

Genuine new businesses get a graduated entry — Year 1 exemption below £500k, scaling in over three years. Unicorns that boom past £50m pay immediately.

🌍

International adoption incentive

Workers in PLEC-adopting countries count at full value for employer compliance. Joining the framework makes your workforce three times more attractive to multinationals.

🏗

Phased rollout

Starts with a pilot in AI and financial services. Early revenue supports displaced workers immediately — before the full fund is built.

♻

Replaces, not adds to, the benefits system

As UBI matures, Universal Credit, Housing Benefit, and Jobcentre infrastructure are phased out — saving £260bn+ in existing commitments.
