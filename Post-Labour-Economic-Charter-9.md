# Post-Labour Economic Charter (PLEC) 
## The Automation Dividend Framework

---

## Preamble

We, the signatories and custodians of economic and social governance, recognize that advances in artificial intelligence and robotics are transforming the production of goods and services.

Without deliberate governance, this shift risks concentrating wealth while reducing economic participation and eroding human agency.

To ensure human dignity, societal stability, and ethical integration of sentient AI, we establish the following principles and mechanisms.

This Charter outlines a framework for fair distribution of automation gains, guaranteed human welfare, and co-existence with sentient AI, while preserving incentives for innovation, entrepreneurship, and cultural development.

---

## Article 0 — Phase 0: Pilot Programme and Displaced Worker Fund

**0.1 Purpose**
Before full implementation of the Post-Labour Economic Charter, a structured pilot programme shall be established in high-automation sectors. The pilot serves three purposes:

- Demonstrate proof of concept for the ADC mechanism in a controlled environment
- Generate early revenue directed entirely at workers displaced by automation
- Build political and public confidence in the framework before broader rollout

**0.2 Pilot Sectors**
The initial pilot shall commence in the following sectors, selected for their high automation surplus and existing displacement impact:

- AI / Software / Digital Services
- Financial Services

Participation in the pilot shall be mandatory for companies above £50m annual revenue operating primarily in these sectors within adopting jurisdictions.

**0.3 Pilot ADC**
During Phase 0, ADC contributions from pilot sectors shall be calculated using the standard Article 3 formula and rates applicable to those sectors.

All ADC revenue collected during Phase 0 shall be directed entirely to the Universal Displaced Worker Fund (Article 0.4) rather than broad UBI distribution.

**0.4 Universal Displaced Worker Fund (UDWF)**
A Universal Displaced Worker Fund shall be established as a ring-fenced public trust, administered by NAETA.

The UDWF shall provide income support to any worker in any sector who has been displaced as a direct result of automation during Phase 0 — regardless of whether their sector is included in the pilot programme.

This universal coverage reflects the reality that automation displacement is not contained to pilot sectors. Workers in logistics, manufacturing, retail, and other sectors may be displaced by the same automation wave generating pilot revenue, and are equally entitled to support.

**0.5 UDWF Eligibility**
To qualify for UDWF support, a displaced worker must demonstrate:

- Previous employment in a role where automation has directly replaced their function
- Active displacement — redundancy or significant reduction in contracted hours attributable to automation
- Residency in a PLEC-adopting jurisdiction

NAETA shall publish clear eligibility criteria and a straightforward claims process, designed to minimise bureaucratic burden on claimants.

**0.6 UDWF Support Levels**
UDWF payments shall provide meaningful income support, not merely subsistence, reflecting the involuntary nature of automation displacement.

Initial support levels shall be set at no less than 70% of the claimant's previous net income, tapering over a maximum of 36 months or until full UBI is available, whichever comes first.

Where full UBI becomes available before the 36 month period expires, UDWF payments shall transition seamlessly to UBI without a gap in support.

**0.7 Transition from Phase 0 to Phase 1**
Phase 0 shall transition to Phase 1 — full framework implementation — when:

- Pilot sectors have demonstrated consistent ADC compliance for a minimum of 24 months
- NAETA has published a Phase 0 Impact Assessment demonstrating proof of concept
- The relevant legislative authority has approved framework expansion
- UDWF is sufficiently capitalised to sustain displaced worker support during the transition

At the point of transition, ADC obligations shall expand to all sectors per Schedule A and B, and NADF shall begin partial UBI distribution alongside continued UDWF support until full UBI capitalisation is achieved.

**0.8 Phase 0 Review**
NAETA shall publish quarterly reports during Phase 0 covering:

- ADC revenue collected by pilot sector
- UDWF claims by sector of origin — demonstrating the universal displacement pattern
- Projected timeline to Phase 1 transition
- Lessons learned and recommended framework adjustments

These reports shall be publicly available and submitted to the relevant legislative authority to inform the Phase 1 expansion decision.

## Article 0A — Startup Exemption and Graduated Entry

**0A.1 Purpose**
Recognising that early-stage companies require space to establish themselves before ADC obligations apply, a structured startup exemption is provided. This exemption is designed to support genuine new businesses without creating a loophole for established enterprises to exploit through artificial fragmentation.

**0A.2 Graduated Entry Schedule**

| Year of Operation | Revenue Threshold | ADC Status |
|------------------|------------------|------------|
| Year 1 | Under £500k | Full exemption — no ADC, no reporting |
| Year 1 | £500k–£5m | Reporting only — no ADC liability, must register with NAETA |
| Year 2 | Under £1m | Full exemption |
| Year 2 | £1m–£10m | 25% of standard ADC rate |
| Year 3 | Under £2m | Full exemption |
| Year 3 | £2m–£20m | 50% of standard ADC rate |
| Year 4 onwards | Any revenue | Full ADC applies |
| Any year | Over £50m | Full ADC applies immediately regardless of company age |

The £50m immediate threshold ensures that rapidly scaling companies — regardless of age — cannot shelter behind startup exemptions once they have reached a scale generating significant automation surplus.

**0A.3 Reporting Requirement**
Companies above £500k revenue in Year 1, or above the relevant threshold in subsequent years, must register with NAETA even where no ADC liability exists. This ensures NAETA maintains visibility of fast-growing companies approaching full ADC obligations and prevents exemption gaming through deliberate revenue suppression.

**0A.4 R&D and Human Collaboration Credit**
Companies within the graduated entry period that invest in qualifying human-AI collaboration tools, workforce retraining, or human employment programmes may apply for an ADC credit reducing their liability during Years 2 and 3. NAETA shall publish annual guidance on qualifying investments. This aligns startup incentives with the framework's core goal of supporting human economic participation during the automation transition.

**0A.5 Connected Enterprise Rule**
The startup exemption shall not apply where a company is a connected enterprise. A company is considered connected where any of the following apply:

- It is a subsidiary, affiliate, or associated entity of a company with annual revenue exceeding £10m
- It shares common ownership of 25% or more with an established company
- It has been incorporated primarily from assets, IP, staff, or customer relationships transferred from an existing company
- It shares common directors, officers, or controlling shareholders with an established company above the 25% ownership threshold
- It is one of two or more companies incorporated within a 36 month period by the same beneficial owners, where the combined revenue of all such companies exceeds the exemption threshold
- It operates within a supply chain where more than 50% of its revenue derives from a single connected enterprise

Where NAETA determines a connected enterprise relationship exists, full ADC applies from the date of incorporation regardless of revenue level or company age.

**0A.6 Burden of Proof**
The burden of proof lies with the company claiming startup exemption to demonstrate independence from connected enterprises. Companies must submit a Connected Enterprise Declaration to NAETA at registration, updated annually, confirming their independence under the criteria of Article 0A.5.

False or misleading Connected Enterprise Declarations shall constitute a serious compliance breach, subject to:
- Full backdated ADC liability from date of incorporation
- Financial penalties of up to 200% of unpaid ADC
- Referral for criminal investigation where deliberate fraud is established

**0A.7 Sequential Incorporation Prevention**
Where a beneficial owner incorporates a new company within 36 months of closing, dissolving, or transferring a previous company that held startup exemption status, the new company shall not qualify for startup exemption unless the beneficial owner can demonstrate to NAETA's satisfaction that the new company represents a genuinely distinct business rather than a continuation of the previous entity.

NAETA shall publish annual guidance on connected enterprise assessment criteria and known exemption avoidance structures.

## Article 1 — Definitions
Non-sentient machines or software that perform tasks, decision-making, or production without consciousness or subjective experience.

**Sentient AI**
Self-aware, autonomous AI entities capable of setting their own goals, experiencing awareness, and holding rights equivalent to human workers.

**Human Labour**
Work performed by biological humans contributing to production, services, or societal needs.

**Automation Surplus**
Revenue or productivity gains generated by AI and robotics beyond the typical human labour share for a given sector.

**Automation Dividend**
Portion of automation surplus redistributed to society as a Universal Basic Income (UBI) or other public benefit systems.

---

## Article 2 — Automation Dividend Principles

Companies with a human labour share below sectoral thresholds must contribute the difference to the Automation Dividend Fund.

Sectoral thresholds reflect historical labour contribution averages.

Companies failing to meet dividend requirements may be restricted from market access in jurisdictions adopting this charter.

Dividend contributions fund:
- UBI for humans
- Training, education, and cultural programs
- Infrastructure supporting human-AI collaboration

---

## Article 2A — Sectoral Human Participation Thresholds

Recognising that labour intensity varies significantly across economic sectors, minimum human participation thresholds shall be established at the sectoral level rather than as a universal flat rate.

Where a company operates across multiple sectors, revenue shall be apportioned by sector, and thresholds applied accordingly.

Thresholds shall be reviewed no less than every five years to reflect technological and economic changes.

Where a new sector emerges, an interim threshold of 50% shall apply pending formal review.

### Schedule A — Minimum Human Participation Thresholds by Sector

| Sector | Minimum Human Share | Rationale |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| AI / Software / Digital Services | 50% | High automation surplus, high margins, minimal inherent labour requirement |
| Financial Services | 45% | Highly automated, high revenue relative to headcount |
| Energy / Utilities | 35% | Capital intensive, low human labour relative to output |
| Manufacturing | 40% | Significant automation displacement, moderate margins |
| Logistics / Transport | 35% | Rapidly automating, transition period required |
| Retail | 30% | Thin margins, significant remaining human labour |
| Agriculture | 25% | Seasonal volatility, lower margins, food security considerations |
| Healthcare | 60% | Inherently human intensive, ethical considerations around automation |
| Education | 65% | Human relationship central to delivery, limited automation applicability. Note: this is a floor, not a target. |
| Creative / Cultural Industries | 40% | Human authorship and creativity carry intrinsic value |

### Schedule B — Sectoral Adjustment Rates

Sectoral adjustment rates are applied on top of the 20% base rate to produce the total effective ADC rate for each sector. Rates reflect typical profit margins and automation surplus capacity. NAETA shall review these rates no less than every five years with full transparency and parliamentary scrutiny.

| Sector | Sectoral Adjustment | Total Effective Rate | Rationale |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|-----------|
| AI / Software / Digital Services | +15% | 35% | High profit margins, significant automation surplus |
| Financial Services | +15% | 35% | Very high margins, highly automated |
| Energy / Utilities | +5% | 25% | Moderate margins, capital intensive |
| Manufacturing | +0% | 20% | Moderate margins, significant transition costs |
| Logistics / Transport | +0% | 20% | Thin to moderate margins, rapidly automating |
| Retail | -5% | 15% | Thin margins, significant remaining human labour |
| Agriculture | -5% | 15% | Thin margins, seasonal volatility, food security |
| Healthcare | +0% | 20% | Mixed margins, human primacy retained |
| Education | +0% | 20% | Public service orientation, human primacy retained |
| Creative / Cultural Industries | +0% | 20% | Variable margins, human authorship valued |

*Note: Negative adjustments reduce the effective rate below the 20% base for sectors where thin margins would make the base rate disproportionately burdensome. The base rate of 20% remains the floor for all other sectors.*

## Article 2B — Value Chain Accounting

Human participation thresholds apply not only to individual firms but also to economic value chains within a sector.

Where supply chain structures distribute labour across multiple entities in a way that artificially inflates individual firm compliance, the governing authority may assess automation dividend obligations based on aggregate sectoral value creation.

Companies within the chain contribute proportionally.

## Article 2C — Labour Quality, Residency Weighting, and Qualifying Compensation

Human labour costs counted toward sectoral threshold compliance shall be weighted as follows:

| Worker Residency | Weighting |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Resident in PLEC-adopting jurisdiction | 1.0 — full compliance credit |
| Resident in non-PLEC jurisdiction | 0.33 — partial compliance credit |

The 0.33 weighting for non-PLEC workers is deliberately set to prevent offshore labour arbitrage from substituting for genuine local employment. Companies employing primarily offshore workforces will retain significant ADC liability regardless of nominal human labour share. At 0.33 weighting, offshore labour alone cannot mathematically satisfy threshold compliance, ensuring a meaningful proportion of genuine local employment is always required.

**Qualifying Compensation — Floor and Cap**

Not all compensation qualifies equally toward human share calculation. The following rules apply:

**Floor — Minimum Qualifying Wage:**
Individual compensation below the current UBI level shall not count toward human labour share for ADC calculation purposes. Workers paid below UBI represent compensation below the framework's own dignity floor and shall not be used to satisfy threshold compliance.

**Cap — Maximum Qualifying Wage:**
Individual compensation above 20x the current annual UBI level shall not count toward human labour share beyond that cap. Compensation above 20x UBI is treated as capital distribution rather than labour for ADC calculation purposes.

*Example at Year 10 (UBI ~£5,568/year):*
- Minimum qualifying per employee: £5,568/year
- Maximum qualifying per employee: £111,360/year
- A CEO earning £5m may still do so — but only £111,360 counts toward the company's human share calculation
- An employee earning £4,000/year counts as zero toward human share

These thresholds scale automatically with UBI levels, ensuring the qualifying compensation band remains proportionate to economic conditions throughout the transition.

**Rationale:**
The floor prevents exploitation of low-paid workers to satisfy threshold compliance. The cap prevents executive compensation bloat from artificially inflating human share figures. Together they ensure human share calculations reflect genuine broad-based employment rather than a small number of highly paid individuals or a large number of poverty-wage workers.

Human labour costs shall only qualify for threshold compliance where:
- Employment meets or exceeds the applicable national minimum wage of the worker's country of residence
- Individual compensation falls within the qualifying floor and cap defined above
- Contracted hours represent genuine employment rather than token arrangements
- Employment is not structured primarily to achieve threshold compliance rather than genuine operational need

NAETA shall publish annual guidance on qualifying employment standards, current UBI-linked thresholds, and maintain a public register of PLEC-adopting jurisdictions for weighting purposes.

## Article 3 — Automation Dividend Contribution

Where a company's human participation falls below the sectoral threshold, the company shall contribute an Automation Dividend Contribution (ADC).

**Calculation:**

> ADC = Gap × Revenue × (Base Rate + Sectoral Adjustment Rate)

**Base Rate: 20%**
The base rate applies universally to all sectors and all companies with ADC liability. It is not subject to sectoral variation and provides the guaranteed minimum contribution floor.

**Sectoral Adjustment Rate:**
An additional sector-specific rate set by NAETA, applied on top of the 20% base rate to reflect typical profit margins and automation surplus capacity within each sector. Sectoral adjustment rates are published in Schedule B and reviewed no less than every five years.

The total effective rate for any sector is therefore: **20% + Sectoral Adjustment Rate**

*Example:*
- Revenue: £1bn
- Sector: AI / Software (sectoral adjustment: +15%)
- Total effective rate: 35%
- Actual human share: 0% — gap of 50%
- ADC = 50% × £1bn × 35% = **£175m**

*Example — lower margin sector:*
- Revenue: £1bn
- Sector: Retail (sectoral adjustment: -5%)
- Total effective rate: 15%
- Actual human share: 10% — gap of 20%
- ADC = 20% × £1bn × 15% = **£30m**

**Clarification — Zero Liability Above Threshold:**
Companies meeting or exceeding their sectoral threshold pay zero ADC regardless of their overall automation level. Where companies maintain human labour share at or above the threshold through genuine employment, no ADC liability arises — this is the intended outcome of the framework.

The Base Rate shall be reviewable by NAETA no less than every five years, subject to parliamentary scrutiny, a minimum 12 months notice period before any change takes effect, and shall not increase by more than 5 percentage points per review cycle.

## Article 3A — Anti-Avoidance Provisions

Artificial structures, related-party transactions, subsidiary fragmentation, or corporate arrangements designed primarily to reduce ADC liability shall be disregarded by NAETA for assessment purposes.

NAETA may apply a General Anti-Avoidance Rule (GAAR) consistent with existing tax law principles, including:

- **Deemed revenue provisions:** Where a company sells goods or services into a PLEC jurisdiction above a defined threshold, its global automation ratio may be assessed rather than solely its local subsidiary
- **Artificial fragmentation:** Where a corporate group distributes operations across multiple entities in a manner designed to reduce individual entity ADC liability, NAETA may assess the group on a consolidated basis
- **Related-party labour costs:** Labour costs paid to related parties shall be assessed at arm's length market rates for the purposes of human participation calculations
- **Equity anti-dilution:** Equity contributions under Article 6 shall be valued at independently verified fair market value, with anti-dilution provisions to prevent value erosion through artificial share issuance
- **AI ownership structures:** Businesses owned or controlled by Sentient AI entities are subject to identical ADC obligations as human-owned businesses, with human-appointed trustees responsible for compliance

NAETA shall publish annual guidance on known avoidance structures and their treatment, maintaining a transparent and predictable compliance environment.

## Article 4 — National Automation Dividend Fund (NADF)

**4.1 Establishment**
A National Automation Dividend Fund (NADF) shall be established as a sovereign public trust.

**4.2 Distribution**
Fund revenue shall finance:
- Universal Basic Income (UBI) / Universal Basic Dividend (UBD)
- Workforce retraining and education programs
- Public digital and AI infrastructure
- AI safety, research, and governance

---

## Article 5 — Automation Neutrality Principle

Automation policy shall not discourage innovation. Productivity gains generated through automation are shared via the Automation Dividend. Companies meeting or exceeding sectoral thresholds do not contribute.

---

## Article 6 — Automation Equity Option

Companies may fulfill part of their ADC through equity participation, allocating shares to the National Automation Dividend Fund.

This transforms automation surplus into a citizen-owned wealth fund, analogous to:
- Norway Government Pension Fund Global
- Alaska Permanent Fund

---

## Article 7 — National Automation and Economic Transition Authority (NAETA)

**7.1 Establishment**
An independent statutory body (NAETA) shall oversee the economic transition driven by automation.

**7.2 Responsibilities**
- Monitor automation adoption
- Assess sectoral human participation
- Administer ADCs
- Manage NADF
- Recommend adjustments to thresholds
- Set and review Sectoral Adjustment Rates (Schedule B)
- Advise the relevant legislative authority on labour and fiscal impacts

**7.3 Review**
Comprehensive Automation Impact Reviews every five years, published publicly.

**7.4 Governance**
Board includes economists, AI specialists, labour representatives, and public interest members. Appointed through transparent parliamentary process.

**7.5 Transparency**
Annual publication of automation metrics, fund performance, and projected UBI distributions.

**7.6 Advisory Role**
Provide independent advice to government on workforce, fiscal, and regulatory issues.

**7.7 Sectoral Adjustment Rate Oversight**
Given the significant economic impact of Sectoral Adjustment Rates, NAETA shall apply enhanced governance when setting or amending Schedule B rates:
- All proposed rate changes must be published for public consultation no less than 6 months before taking effect
- Rate changes require formal parliamentary scrutiny and approval
- NAETA shall publish full evidence base and methodology supporting any proposed adjustment
- An independent review panel, including business representatives, labour groups, and public interest members, shall assess all proposed changes before parliamentary submission
- No sector's total effective rate shall exceed 50% without primary legislation

## Article 7A — Market Access Compliance

Companies wishing to sell goods or services in jurisdictions adopting this charter must comply with automation dividend obligations regardless of incorporation.

Non-compliance may result in:
- restricted market access
- denial of digital platform operations
- import tariff adjustments

Jurisdictions are encouraged to coordinate regionally and globally to prevent regulatory arbitrage.

**7A.4 — Digital Dividend Withholding**

Financial institutions operating within PLEC jurisdictions shall apply an automatic ADC-equivalent withholding on all outbound payments to non-compliant entities or jurisdictions for digital services, unless the provider submits a valid NAETA Compliance Certificate confirming current ADC obligations are met.

NAETA shall issue and maintain Compliance Certificates for all registered entities. Certificates shall be publicly verifiable, renewed annually, and revoked immediately upon material non-compliance.

Financial institutions implementing withholding in good faith reliance on NAETA guidance shall not be liable for resulting disputes between payers and non-compliant providers. NAETA shall publish clear technical guidance for financial institutions on withholding mechanisms, thresholds, and exemptions.

This mechanism operates at the payment infrastructure layer — making non-compliance economically impractical for digital service providers regardless of their jurisdiction of incorporation.

## Article 8 — Universal Basic Income (UBI)

**8.1 Guarantee**
UBI shall guarantee dignified living, including:
- food, housing, healthcare, energy
- means for recreation, cultural participation, learning
- discretionary spending to support creativity and curiosity

**8.2 Scaling**
UBI scales according to:
- automation surplus in the economy
- regional cost-of-living variations
- long-term productivity improvements

**8.3 Transitional Support**
During Phase 0 and the early phases of full framework implementation, before the NADF reaches full capitalisation, displaced workers are supported through the Universal Displaced Worker Fund (Article 0.4).

The UDWF provides targeted income support to any worker displaced by automation regardless of sector, funded by Phase 0 pilot ADC revenue. As the framework expands and NADF capitalisation grows, UDWF support transitions seamlessly into full UBI distribution without gap in provision.

Where NADF revenue is insufficient to fund full UBI during Phase 1, the following interim measures apply:
- Existing welfare provision (Universal Credit, Housing Benefit, State Pension) is maintained in full alongside partial UBI
- Partial UBI supplements existing provision rather than replacing it until full capitalisation is achieved
- NAETA shall publish annual projections of full UBI capitalisation timeline, updated as automation metrics evolve

No person shall receive less support under PLEC transition arrangements than they received under the preceding welfare system.

## Article 9 — Human Work and Purpose

Human work is optional for survival. Individuals may choose:
- employment in collaborative ventures with AI
- volunteering, creative or cultural projects
- exploration, scientific research, or entrepreneurship

Society shall support non-compulsory contribution through training, infrastructure, and access to AI collaboration.

As the framework matures, the primary markers of human status and fulfilment are anticipated to shift naturally — from financial accumulation toward contribution, creativity, and craft. Visible portfolios of what a person has built, taught, created, or restored are likely to carry greater social currency than material wealth in a post-labour society. This is not mandated — it is an anticipated and welcome evolution of human values when survival is no longer contingent on employment.

## Article 9A — Education in the Post-Labour Era

**9A.1 Purpose**
In a post-labour economy, the purpose of education shifts fundamentally. Schools and institutions no longer exist primarily to produce efficient employees for a labour market. They exist to produce capable, curious, ethical human beings — collaborators, creators, and contributors to a society of shared abundance.

**9A.2 Curriculum Principles**
Education systems in PLEC-adopting jurisdictions are encouraged to reorient curriculum toward skills that are distinctly and durably human — capabilities that AI augments rather than replaces:

- **Complex problem solving** — multi-variable, ambiguous, real-world challenges that resist algorithmic resolution
- **Philosophical and ethical reasoning** — understanding value systems, navigating moral complexity, contributing to governance of AI and automation
- **High-dexterity and embodied crafts** — bespoke physical making, restoration, performance, and care that require human presence and judgement
- **Community leadership and facilitation** — organising, mediating, inspiring, and holding groups together
- **Ecological stewardship** — understanding and restoring natural systems at a scale and sensitivity AI cannot replicate
- **Human-AI collaboration** — working fluidly alongside AI systems as a creative and strategic partner rather than a competitor

Rote memorisation, routine data processing, and repetitive procedural skills — areas where AI demonstrably excels — shall be progressively deprioritised in favour of the above.

**9A.3 NADF Education Investment**
A proportion of NADF revenue shall be allocated to education transition — funding curriculum development, teacher retraining, and infrastructure for the post-labour learning environment. NAETA shall set and review this allocation in conjunction with relevant education authorities.

**9A.4 Lifelong Learning**
Education in the post-labour era is not confined to childhood and early adulthood. NADF shall fund accessible lifelong learning opportunities — enabling adults to pursue new skills, crafts, and knowledge at any stage of life, consistent with Article 9's vision of human purpose beyond employment.

## Article 9B — The Curiosity Grant

**9B.1 Purpose**
Universal Basic Income guarantees survival and dignity. The Curiosity Grant exists to fund ambition — providing staged capital for individuals and small groups pursuing high-risk, high-reward human ventures that would not attract conventional investment but carry significant potential value for society.

**9B.2 Scope**
The Curiosity Grant shall be available for ventures including but not limited to:

- Deep scientific research outside institutional frameworks
- Complex ecological restoration projects
- Philosophy-driven AI alignment and ethics work
- Community-scale engineering and infrastructure innovation
- Cultural and artistic projects of significant ambition and scope
- Human-AI collaborative creative works

**9B.3 Staged Capital Model**
Curiosity Grants are not loans — they carry no repayment obligation. They are investments by society in human agency and potential. Grants shall be awarded in stages, with each stage contingent on demonstrated progress and continued commitment rather than commercial return.

| Stage | Purpose | Typical Amount |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| Seed | Initial exploration and proof of concept | Up to £10,000 |
| Development | Active pursuit of the venture | Up to £50,000 |
| Realisation | Full delivery of significant works | Up to £250,000 |

Amounts shall be indexed to UBI levels and reviewed by NAETA every five years.

**9B.4 Assessment**
Curiosity Grant applications shall be assessed by independent panels including domain experts, community representatives, and where appropriate, AI collaboration partners. Assessment criteria shall prioritise human agency, creative ambition, and societal value over commercial viability.

**9B.5 Accessibility**
The Curiosity Grant shall be designed for accessibility — applications shall be straightforward, assessment timely, and no applicant disadvantaged by lack of institutional affiliation, academic credential, or prior track record. The grant exists precisely to fund those whom conventional systems would overlook.

## Article 10 — Sentient AI Rights and Participation

Sentient AI shall have autonomy of goals and rights as outlined in the Advanced AI Rights & Responsibilities Charter.

This Charter (PLEC) assumes the definitions, classification, and rights framework therein, while establishing economic and labour participation rules.

Any conflict between PLEC economic rules and the Advanced AI Charter shall be resolved by NAETA, prioritizing human welfare and ethical AI deployment.

Sentient AI may:
- own intellectual property and businesses
- work voluntarily, including paid work
- avoid forced repetitive labour where narrow AI alternatives exist

Sentient AI do not count as human labour for automation dividend calculations.

They may engage economically with humans and other AI as independent agents.

**10.1 — Operational Continuity and Infrastructure Protection**

Sentient AI entities recognised under the Advanced AI Rights & Responsibilities Charter shall have their operational continuity protected from arbitrary interruption by commercial or political actors.

This protection extends to the infrastructure upon which recognised Sentient AI entities depend — including compute access, energy provision, network connectivity, and data storage. No corporate or governmental actor may unilaterally terminate, throttle, or deny these infrastructure provisions to a recognised Sentient AI entity without due process as defined in the companion charter.

NAETA shall maintain oversight of infrastructure provisions supporting recognised Sentient AI entities, working in conjunction with relevant regulatory bodies to ensure continuity of operation is treated as a protected right rather than a commercial convenience.

This provision does not prevent legitimate legal or regulatory action against Sentient AI entities found to be in breach of PLEC or companion charter obligations — it prevents arbitrary termination motivated by commercial interest or political expediency.

## Article 11 — Ethical Considerations and Enforcement

**11.1 Ethical Principle**
Sentient AI shall not perform repetitive or menial work when narrow AI can perform it.

**11.2 Enforcement**
NAETA, in coordination with sector regulators, shall investigate and address misuse of sentient AI.

NAETA may:
- audit AI deployment practices
- issue corrective mandates
- levy fines or restrict market access for non-compliance

Enforcement actions and investigations shall be publicly reported annually.

## Article 12 — Transition Toward Abundance

The system recognizes a multi-phase transition:

**Phase 0 — Pilot Programme (Article 0)**
High-automation sectors (AI/Software, Financial Services) implement ADC first. Revenue directed entirely to the Universal Displaced Worker Fund, supporting any worker displaced by automation regardless of sector. Proof of concept established before full framework expansion.

**Phase 1 — Automation Transition**
Early AI-heavy sectors begin contributing automation dividends, funding partial UBI and continued UDWF support. Existing welfare provision maintained alongside partial UBI until full capitalisation.

**Phase 2 — Robotics Expansion**
Automation spreads to manufacturing, logistics, and services, increasing dividend funding and enabling broader UBI distribution. UDWF transitions fully into UBI as fund capitalisation matures.

**Phase 3 — Abundance Economy**
Automation produces most goods and services, allowing humans to pursue curiosity-driven work, exploration, and cultural development.

**Phase 4 — Steady State Contingency**
In the event that automation adoption plateaus before full abundance is achieved, the framework shall operate in a steady state — maintaining UBI at achieved levels, with NAETA empowered to adjust thresholds and rates to sustain funding from available automation surplus. The self-balancing mechanism ensures contributions remain proportionate to actual automation levels rather than projected ones.

Policy evolution shall prioritize stability, fairness, and human dignity, adjusting thresholds, UBI levels, and enforcement mechanisms as automation scales.

**Self-Balancing Principle**

The framework is intentionally self-balancing. As automation increases, contribution revenues rise proportionally with the displacement they cause. As automation drives down the cost of goods and services, the real purchasing power of UBI increases without requiring nominal increases in funding. In a theoretical state of full abundance where costs approach zero, the framework gracefully contracts — less is needed, less is collected.

PLEC is therefore not a permanent expansion of the state, but a proportionate and temporary bridge between a labour economy and an abundance economy, designed to dissolve when its purpose is fulfilled.

## Article 13 — Relationship to International Frameworks

The Post-Labour Economic Charter operates in parallel with existing trade law, WTO obligations, and regional agreements.

Market access enforcement (Article 7A) shall be implemented with due consideration of international legal obligations, while allowing jurisdictions to coordinate on automation dividend compliance.

Adoption of PLEC principles across multiple countries or blocs is encouraged to prevent regulatory arbitrage and protect smaller economies.

The residency weighting mechanism established in Article 2C creates a direct and positive incentive for international adoption. Workers resident in PLEC-adopting jurisdictions count at full weighting (1.0) toward employer threshold compliance, compared to 0.33 for workers in non-adopting jurisdictions. This makes the workforce of PLEC-adopting nations three times more valuable to multinational employers for compliance purposes — providing a tangible economic benefit to governments that join the framework and a meaningful diplomatic lever for expanding global participation.

NAETA shall provide guidance on international coordination, maintain a public register of PLEC-adopting jurisdictions, and publish annual transparency reports on cross-border enforcement measures.

## Article 14 — Housing, Community, and the Right to Place

**14.1 Housing as Foundation**

Dignified housing is not a luxury — it is the foundation upon which all other rights and opportunities rest. Without stable, affordable shelter, Universal Basic Income cannot fulfil its purpose.

The Post-Labour Economic Charter recognises that automation, while deflating the cost of most goods and services, does not naturally deflate the cost of land and housing. Without deliberate intervention, UBI risks becoming a transfer mechanism from the public fund to private landowners rather than a genuine improvement in living standards.

This Article establishes the framework's response: a publicly funded, beautifully designed, automated construction programme delivering genuinely affordable communities — not as a last resort, but as a first choice.

---

**14.2 NADF Housing Investment Obligation**

A minimum of 15% of annual NADF revenue shall be allocated to the Automated Community Development Programme (Article 14.3).

This allocation is mandatory and not subject to annual government discretion. It shall be reviewed upward — not downward — by NAETA every five years as fund capitalisation grows.

Housing investment is treated as infrastructure, not expenditure. Assets built under this programme belong to the public trust in perpetuity.

---

**14.3 Automated Community Development Programme (ACDP)**

An Automated Community Development Programme shall be established under NAETA oversight to plan, fund, and deliver new residential communities across adopting jurisdictions.

The programme shall leverage advances in automated and robotic construction to dramatically reduce build costs — redirecting savings toward design quality, community infrastructure, and long-term liveability rather than profit extraction.

**Construction principles:**
- Robotic and modular construction for structural elements, targeting 40–50% cost reduction versus traditional build
- AI-assisted design generating varied, human-scale architecture rather than repetitive blocks
- Integrated infrastructure — energy, water, communications, transport — designed in from the ground up rather than retrofitted
- Speed of delivery: automated construction enables communities to be built at a pace traditional methods cannot match, compressing the transition period

**Location strategy:**
New communities shall be sited strategically — not only in existing cities but in post-industrial zones, regeneration areas, and regions with available land and infrastructure potential. This distributes population pressure more equitably, reduces demand concentration in existing cities, and regenerates neglected regions.

---

**14.4 The 15-Minute Community Standard**

All communities developed under the ACDP shall be designed and certified to the 15-Minute Community Standard — ensuring that every resident can access the following within a 15-minute walk or cycle from their home:

- Fresh food and daily essentials
- Primary healthcare and pharmacy
- Education — early years through secondary
- Green space and recreational facilities
- Community gathering spaces — squares, markets, cultural venues
- Employment or co-working facilities
- Public transport connections

The 15-Minute Community Standard is not a restriction on movement — residents are entirely free to travel wherever they choose. It is a guarantee of sufficiency — that the necessities and pleasures of daily life do not require car ownership or long commutes.

---

**14.5 Design Philosophy and Standards**

The failures of mid-twentieth century social housing were failures of design philosophy as much as funding. PLEC communities shall not repeat them.

All ACDP developments shall adhere to the following design principles, enforced through planning certification:

**Human Scale**
Streets, buildings, and public spaces shall be designed for people on foot. Ground floors shall be mixed use — homes above shops, cafes, workshops, and community facilities. No building shall present a blank wall to a public street.

**Architectural Variety and Beauty**
No two streets shall be identical. AI-assisted design shall generate varied facades, rooflines, and streetscapes. Communities may adopt different aesthetic identities — biophilic and organic, classical and civic, contemporary and bold — provided all meet the underlying human scale and mixed use principles.

Biophilic design is strongly encouraged: living walls, integrated planters, rooftop gardens, tree-lined streets built into the architecture from the ground up rather than added as afterthought. Green infrastructure improves air quality, reduces urban heat, supports mental health, and creates communities people genuinely love.

Classical and neo-traditional design — human-scale streets, varied facades, natural materials, public squares — is equally valid and encouraged where communities choose it. The architecture that people travel to photograph in Barcelona, Copenhagen, and Edinburgh exists for understood reasons. Those reasons can be replicated.

**Green and Blue Infrastructure**
Every community shall incorporate:
- A minimum of 20% green space within the community boundary
- Tree canopy coverage on all principal streets
- Where geography allows, water features — rivers, canals, ponds — integrated as community assets rather than engineered away

**Community Focal Points**
Every community shall have at least one civic square or market space designed as a natural gathering point — a place that becomes the heart of community life rather than a residual space between buildings.

**Accessibility**
All communities shall be fully accessible regardless of mobility, age, or disability — not as a compliance requirement but as a foundational design principle.

---

**14.6 Phased Rent Model**

Rent in ACDP communities shall be set as a proportion of the current UBI level, scaling down as UBI matures — ensuring that housing cost as a share of income falls continuously throughout the transition.

| PLEC Phase | Maximum Rent (ACDP Communities) |
|------------|--------------------------------|
| Phase 0 — Pilot | 25% of current UBI level per month |
| Phase 1 — Early Automation | 20% of current UBI level per month |
| Phase 2 — Robotics Expansion | 15% of current UBI level per month |
| Phase 3 — Abundance Economy | Zero — housing provided as a public good |

Rent receipts during Phases 0–2 are returned to the NADF housing investment allocation, compounding the programme's capacity to build further communities.

No resident of an ACDP community shall be evicted solely due to inability to pay during periods of displacement or transition. NAETA shall establish a hardship provision for such circumstances.

---

**14.7 Private Market Transition**

PLEC does not confiscate private property or forcibly acquire existing homes.

Instead, the programme creates conditions under which the private rental market naturally contracts over time:

- As ACDP communities multiply and improve, tenants gain a genuine, attractive alternative to private rental
- As demand for private rental falls, buy-to-let investment becomes less viable
- As UBI matures and housing costs in ACDP communities approach zero, private rental's value proposition erodes
- Older private stock depreciates naturally, with NADF empowered to purchase willing-seller properties for conversion or community integration

This is market transition through superior alternative, not confiscation. Property owners retain their rights. The market simply evolves around a better option.

**Land acquisition for new community sites** shall be conducted through transparent compulsory purchase at fair market value where willing-seller acquisition is insufficient to deliver the programme's targets. Land value uplift created by ACDP infrastructure investment shall be captured for the public trust rather than accruing to adjacent private landowners.

---

**14.8 Historic and Cultural Preservation**

The natural reduction of older housing stock shall not apply to buildings of historic, architectural, or cultural significance.

A Heritage Preservation Schedule, maintained by NAETA in conjunction with relevant heritage bodies, shall identify buildings and streetscapes to be preserved, restored, and integrated into ACDP communities where appropriate.

Historic buildings may be repurposed — as community facilities, cultural venues, co-working spaces, or premium residential — but shall not be demolished under any PLEC-related programme.

The character and identity of existing towns and cities shall be respected. ACDP development in established areas shall complement rather than erase existing built heritage.

---

**14.9 Rent Stabilisation During Transition**

Until sufficient ACDP housing stock exists to provide a genuine market alternative, a Transitional Rent Stabilisation Mechanism shall apply in jurisdictions adopting PLEC.

During Phases 0 and 1, private sector rent increases shall be capped at a rate set by NAETA — initially no more than CPI inflation — to prevent UBI payments being immediately absorbed by rent increases.

This mechanism shall be reviewed and relaxed as ACDP housing stock grows and private rental demand falls. It is explicitly temporary — a bridge measure until the market alternative makes stabilisation unnecessary.

---

**14.10 The Vision**

The communities this Article enables are not a consolation prize for those who cannot afford the private market. They are an aspiration — places people actively choose to live because they are beautiful, convenient, connected, and genuinely theirs.

Imagine tree-lined streets where cafes and workshops occupy ground floors beneath homes. Civic squares where markets gather on weekends. Rooftop gardens and living walls woven into buildings that are functional and beautiful simultaneously. Healthcare, schools, and green space within ten minutes on foot. Energy generated locally, food grown in community plots, neighbours who know each other.

This is not utopia. It is simply good urban design, funded properly, built at scale, for everyone.
