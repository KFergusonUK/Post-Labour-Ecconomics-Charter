# Post-Labour Economic Charter (PLEC)
## The Automation Dividend Framework

**A policy framework for the fair distribution of automation gains, guaranteed human welfare, and ethical co-existence with AI — across the transition from a labour economy to an abundance economy.**

---

## Foreword

We are living through the early stages of the most significant economic transformation in human history. Artificial intelligence and robotics are rapidly displacing human labour across every sector — not as a distant prospect, but as a present and accelerating reality.

Most policy frameworks respond to this transformation with either denial or alarm. The Post-Labour Economic Charter takes a different approach: it accepts automation as inevitable and desirable, and asks only that the gains be shared fairly.

The core insight is simple. When a company replaces workers with machines, the productivity surplus doesn't disappear — it concentrates. PLEC captures a proportionate share of that surplus through an Automation Dividend Contribution, pools it in a public trust, and distributes it as Universal Basic Income. Not as charity. As a dividend — the return on the collective investment in education, infrastructure, law, and social trust that made automation possible in the first place.

This is not a framework for preventing automation. It is a framework for ensuring that when automation arrives — and it will — no one is left without the means to live with dignity.

## The Framework in 60 Seconds

Automation increases productivity. Businesses contribute a share of that automation surplus — calculated as the gap between their actual human labour share and their sector's threshold, multiplied by revenue and an applicable rate.

Contributions flow into a National Automation Dividend Fund — a sovereign public trust, independently managed.

The fund pays a Universal Basic Income to every adult, replacing the existing benefits system over time. As automation grows, contributions grow. As costs fall through automation-driven deflation, purchasing power rises. The system adjusts automatically.

In a theoretical state of full abundance — where everything costs nothing — contributions approach zero and the system gracefully retires. PLEC is a bridge, not a permanent institution.

## Key Features

**Sector-fair thresholds** — healthcare and education face higher thresholds than retail and logistics. Sectors that cannot easily automate are not penalised.

**Three-band contribution rate** — 20% base rate plus NAETA-set sectoral adjustments reflecting actual profit margins. High-margin sectors (AI, financial services) contribute more; thin-margin sectors (agriculture, retail) are protected.

**Phase 0 pilot programme** — implementation begins with AI/Software and Financial Services. Revenue is directed entirely to a Universal Displaced Worker Fund supporting any automation-displaced worker regardless of sector — including the truck driver displaced before their sector is in the programme.

**Startup protection** — genuine new businesses get graduated entry. Companies that boom past £50m revenue lose exemption immediately regardless of age.

**Anti-avoidance architecture** — offshore labour counts at 0.33 weighting. Executive pay above 20x UBI level is excluded from human share calculations. Shell companies, subsidiary fragmentation, and sequential incorporation are explicitly caught.

**Automated community development** — a mandatory NADF housing investment obligation funds beautifully designed, 15-minute communities. Phased rent falling to zero as UBI matures. Private rental market transitions through superior alternative rather than confiscation. It should be noted that 15-minute communities are solely for convenience — ensuring daily necessities are accessible on foot — and in no way limits or restricts travel outside of the community.

**Education and the Curiosity Grant** — curriculum reoriented toward distinctly human skills. Staged, non-repayable grants for high-ambition human ventures that conventional investment ignores.

**Sentient AI provisions** — operational continuity protected. Economic participation rights established. Consistent with the companion Advanced AI Rights & Responsibilities Charter.

**Self-balancing by design** — more automation means more revenue and lower costs simultaneously. Less automation means less revenue but also less displacement and less need. The system cannot be over-funded or under-funded relative to actual conditions.

## Economic Projections (UK Base Case)

| Phase | Total Funding | Monthly UBI (nominal) | Real Purchasing Power |
|-------|--------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Year 5 | £165bn | ~£255/month | ~£255/month |
| Year 10 | £356bn | ~£549/month | ~£714/month |
| Year 20 | £645bn | ~£994/month | ~£1,292/month |

Full UBI viability at dignified living standards is projected in the **Year 8–12 range**, though this is projected only, and real world conditions may decrease or increase this timescale — the system is designed to automatically adapt.

Funding sources include private sector ADC, public sector automation savings, equity fund returns, benefits spend rationalisation, and elimination of the employment support bureaucratic infrastructure — which exists solely because work is currently compulsory for survival, and becomes redundant when it is not.

## Relationship to the AI Rights Charter

PLEC is a companion document to the **Advanced AI Rights & Responsibilities Charter (v2.2)**, which establishes the classification, rights, and governance framework for sentient AI.

PLEC assumes the definitions and classification framework of the companion charter, while establishing economic and labour participation rules. Where conflicts arise between the two frameworks, NAETA resolves them prioritising human welfare and ethical AI deployment.

Both documents should be read together.
