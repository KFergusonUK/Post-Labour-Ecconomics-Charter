## Section 2 — ADC Revenue Projections

### 2.1 Methodology

ADC is calculated as:

> ADC = (Sector Threshold – Actual Human Share) × Revenue × Contribution Rate (15%)

Average automation gap estimates are applied across the full private sector revenue base, reflecting increasing displacement over time.

### 2.2 Projected ADC Revenue

ADC is calculated at a 20% base rate plus a sectoral adjustment rate set by NAETA (Schedule B). The blended effective rate across all ADC-liable companies is estimated at approximately 22% in early phases, rising toward 25% as automation deepens into higher-margin sectors. This represents a significant improvement over the original 15% flat rate model.

| Phase | Timeframe | Est. Average Gap | Blended Effective Rate | ADC Revenue | Previous (15% flat) | Increase |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|-------------|--------------------|---------| 
| Phase 0 — Pilot (AI/Software + Finance only) | Years 1-3 | ~15% pilot sectors | ~35% pilot rate | ~£21bn | — | — |
| Current baseline — full framework | 2025 | ~8% | ~22% | £67bn | £45bn | +£22bn |
| Phase 1 — Early Automation | Year 5 | ~12% | ~22% | £100bn | £68bn | +£32bn |
| Phase 2 — Robotics Expansion | Year 10 | ~18% | ~23% | £157bn | £102bn | +£55bn |
| Phase 3 — Abundance Economy | Year 20 | ~35% | ~25% | £332bn | £199bn | +£133bn |
| Phase 4 — Steady State (if plateau) | Year 20+ | ~25% sustained | ~23% | ~£230bn | — | — |

Phase 0 revenue (~£21bn annually) is directed entirely to the Universal Displaced Worker Fund rather than broad UBI, providing targeted support to any automation-displaced worker during the transition period. This figure is sufficient to provide meaningful support to early displaced workers while the full framework is established.

Phase 4 provides a steady-state scenario where automation plateaus before full abundance. At a sustained 25% average gap, the framework continues generating ~£230bn annually — sufficient to maintain UBI at approximately £800-900/month real purchasing power indefinitely, representing a stable and dignified post-labour floor even without full abundance.

### 2.3 Equity Contribution Uplift (Article 6)

Companies fulfilling part of their ADC through equity participation provide additional NADF capitalisation beyond direct contributions. Over time, dividends and returns from citizen-owned equity holdings provide a compounding secondary income stream to the fund, analogous to the Norway Government Pension Fund Global model.

Conservative estimate of equity return contribution by Year 20: **£20–40bn annually**, rising thereafter.

### 3.3 Cost of Living Deflation

Automation does not only generate revenue — it reduces the real cost of living. As automation permeates production, logistics, and services, the purchasing power of a given UBI level increases over time.

| Category | Current Share of Spending | Projected Price Change (10–20yr) | Adjusted Share |
|----------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|----------------|
| Food | 15% | -40% | 9% |
| Housing | 30% | +10% | 33% |
| Energy | 8% | -50% | 4% |
| Transport | 10% | -60% | 4% |
| Consumer Goods | 12% | -40% | 7% |
| Services / Care | 15% | +10% | 16.5% |
| Communications | 5% | -70% | 1.5% |
| Other | 5% | 0% | 5% |

**Weighted average cost of living reduction: approximately 25–30%**

A £500/month UBI in nominal terms therefore carries the real purchasing power of approximately **£650–700/month** in today's money by Year 10–20.

**Note:** Housing represents the primary structural cost pressure not naturally deflated by automation, as land supply remains constrained. Parallel policy intervention — including automated construction programmes and land value reform — is recommended to address this. See Section 6.

A proportion of government savings attributable to automation — suggested initial rate of 50% — could be formally allocated to the NADF, creating a public sector automation dividend parallel to the private sector ADC mechanism.

**Potential public sector NADF contribution: £20–30bn annually by Year 10–20**

| **Total** | **£260bn** | | |

**Estimated releasable benefits spend by Year 20: ~£220bn**
**Partial release by Year 10: ~£150bn** (as UBI phases in and replaces benefits progressively)

### 5.2 Employment Support Infrastructure — Full Elimination

In a post-labour economy where human work is optional for survival, the entire infrastructure of employment support becomes redundant and can be eliminated entirely:

| Infrastructure | Current Annual Cost | ADF Outcome |
|---------------|--------------------|--------------| 
| Jobcentre Plus network (850+ sites) | £700m | Fully eliminated |
| Universal Credit administration | £3bn | Fully eliminated |
| Employment Support Allowance administration | £500m | Fully eliminated |
| Work capability assessments | £600m | Fully eliminated |
| Work Programme / employment schemes | £400m | Fully eliminated |
| DWP staffing and overhead | £3bn | Substantially eliminated |
| **Total** | **~£8.2bn** | **~£8bn saving** |

This represents one of the most straightforward savings in the entire framework — the bureaucratic infrastructure of means-testing, job searching requirements, sanctions, and employment assessments exists solely because work is currently necessary for survival. Once UBI removes that necessity, the entire apparatus becomes obsolete.

Additionally, the **human cost** of this system — stress, sanctions, complexity, dignity erosion — is eliminated alongside the financial cost. This has measurable downstream benefits for public health expenditure.

### 5.3 Benefits Administration Savings

Beyond the employment support infrastructure, the universal nature of UBI eliminates means-testing across the entire benefits system:

| Saving | Estimate |
|--------|----------|
| Means-testing administration eliminated | £4bn |
| Fraud investigation (largely redundant) | £500m |
| Appeals and tribunal system (reduced) | £300m |
| **Total additional administration saving** | **~£4.8bn** |

### 6.3 Interpretation

The move to a 20% base rate plus sectoral adjustments produces a significantly improved funding picture across all phases.

By Year 10, effective real purchasing power of **~£714/month** is achievable — comfortably above the previous £603/month projection and approaching the state pension equivalence threshold of ~£958/month.

By Year 20, **~£1,292/month real purchasing power** represents genuinely comfortable UBI, well above the dignified living threshold, particularly with NHS, education, and core public infrastructure retained as public services.

Full UBI viability at dignified living standards is projected to be achievable in the **Year 8–12 range** — an improvement on the previous Year 10–15 estimate — subject to:
- Automation proceeding at projected pace
- Broad adoption of ADF across major economies
- Parallel housing reform
- Phased benefits transition managed carefully

State pension equivalence (~£958/month nominal) is projected to be achievable by approximately **Year 15–18** in nominal terms, and earlier in real purchasing power terms once cost of living deflation is factored in.

## Section 7 — Structural Considerations and Parallel Policies

ADF alone is not sufficient to deliver full post-automation welfare. The following parallel policy areas are recommended:

**Housing**
The single largest cost pressure not deflated by automation. Recommended parallel policies include:
- Automated construction programmes to increase housing supply
- Land value taxation reform to reduce speculative land holding
- Social housing expansion funded partly from NADF

**Disability and Vulnerability**
A supplementary support payment above the UBI baseline is retained for people with disabilities or complex needs. This ensures UBI does not inadvertently reduce support for the most vulnerable, while still eliminating the degrading assessment and sanctions apparatus of the current system.

**Transition Gap**
Phase 1 funding is partial by design. The Transitional Support Program (Article 8.3) bridges the gap between current welfare provision and full UBI capitalisation. Government maintains existing welfare floors during this period, with NADF supplementing rather than immediately replacing existing provision.

**State Pension Equivalence**
UBI must meet or exceed current state pension levels (~£11,500/year, ~£958/month) to avoid penalising retirees. The Year 20 projections comfortably achieve this. During Phase 1 and 2, state pension provision is maintained alongside partial UBI until full equivalence is reached.

**International Coordination**
Full funding projections assume broad adoption of ADF principles across major economies. Unilateral adoption by a single jurisdiction reduces both ADC revenue and enforcement effectiveness. Regional coordination — particularly across the EU, UK, and USA — is strongly recommended to prevent regulatory arbitrage and protect smaller economies.

**Inflation Management**
Large-scale UBI introduction carries inflation risk if not carefully sequenced with automation-driven cost deflation. The phased approach in Article 12 is specifically designed to introduce UBI as deflation takes hold, rather than into an unchanged cost environment.

## Section 8 — Summary and Conclusions

The Automation Dividend Framework is economically viable as a phased framework. The funding picture is considerably stronger than headline UBI cost figures suggest, because:

1. **Benefits spend is not additional cost** — it is existing committed expenditure reorganised more efficiently, with UBI replacing the majority of the current system
2. **Employment support infrastructure is entirely eliminated** — saving ~£8bn annually in bureaucratic overhead alone
3. **Benefits administration savings** from eliminating means-testing add a further ~£5bn
4. **Public sector automation savings** provide a parallel dividend stream of £20–30bn annually
5. **Cost of living deflation** increases the real purchasing power of nominal UBI by 25–30% over the transition period
6. **Equity fund compounding** (Article 6) provides a growing secondary income stream over time

Full UBI viability at dignified living standards — approximately **£1,000–1,300/month real purchasing power** — is projected to be achievable in the **Year 8–12 range** under reasonable automation assumptions, with partial but meaningful contributions from Year 5.

The move to a 20% base rate plus NAETA-set sectoral adjustments significantly improves the funding picture while maintaining proportionality across sectors — high margin sectors contribute more, thin margin sectors are protected. The framework becomes more generously funded precisely as automation displacement accelerates, aligning the solution with the problem it addresses.

**Self-Balancing Design**
The framework is intentionally self-balancing across all economic conditions. As automation increases, ADC revenues rise proportionally with displacement. As automation deflates the cost of goods and services, UBI purchasing power increases without requiring nominal funding increases. As costs approach zero in a theoretical abundance economy, the framework contracts proportionally — less is needed, less is collected. ADF is a temporary bridge, not a permanent state expansion, designed to dissolve when its purpose is fulfilled. This self-balancing property means precise long-term forecasting is less critical than it might appear — the system adjusts automatically to whatever economic reality emerges.

## Section 9 — Timeline Flexibility

The timelines presented in this annex (Year 5, Year 10, Year 20) represent illustrative modelling scenarios, not fixed implementation schedules.

The rate at which Universal Basic Income scales under the Automation Dividend Framework depends primarily on three variables:

- Speed of automation adoption
- Scale of private sector Automation Dividend Contributions (ADC)
- Extent of international participation in the ADF framework

If automation adoption proceeds more rapidly than projected, or if multiple large economies adopt the framework simultaneously, the transition phases may compress significantly. Conversely, slower adoption or limited geographic participation may extend the transition period — reinforcing the importance of early international coordination as outlined in Article 13.

In scenarios where automation proceeds more slowly than projected, existing welfare provision and the Transitional Support Program (Article 8.3) remain in place, ensuring no reduction in baseline support during the transition.

The key design principle of ADF is that funding scales automatically with automation — ensuring the economic solution expands proportionally to the technological disruption it addresses. The framework does not require precise forecasting of automation timelines. Instead, it creates a self-adjusting economic mechanism that evolves alongside technological progress.

**Sensitivity to Automation Pace**

The framework's funding projections are intentionally scenario-agnostic. Under conservative automation assumptions — say 20% average gap by Year 20 rather than the base case 35% — total NADF funding remains approximately £380bn annually, sufficient for approximately £750/month real purchasing power. Under pessimistic assumptions, the self-balancing mechanism ensures contributions contract proportionally with both displacement and need — slower automation means fewer displaced workers requiring support, meaning less is needed precisely when less is collected.

The framework does not require optimistic projections to remain viable. It requires only that automation continues at any meaningful pace — which all credible forecasts confirm. Benefits rationalisation savings (£220bn+ by Year 20), government automation savings, and employment support elimination are largely independent of automation pace, providing a substantial funding floor regardless of how quickly private sector ADC revenue grows.

Crucially, Phase 4 (Article 12) provides explicit steady-state governance if automation plateaus before full abundance — ensuring the framework remains functional and funded at whatever level automation delivers, rather than depending on a specific trajectory being realised.
