## Section 2 — ADC Revenue Projections

### 2.1 Methodology

ADC is calculated as:

> ADC = (Sector Threshold – Actual Human Share) × Revenue × Contribution Rate (15%)

Average automation gap estimates are applied across the full private sector revenue base, reflecting increasing displacement over time.

### 2.2 Projected ADC Revenue

| Phase | Timeframe | Est. Average Gap | ADC Revenue |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| Current baseline | 2025 | ~8% | £45bn |
| Phase 1 — Early Automation | Year 5 | ~12% | £68bn |
| Phase 2 — Robotics Expansion | Year 10 | ~18% | £102bn |
| Phase 3 — Abundance Economy | Year 20 | ~35% | £199bn |

### 2.3 Equity Contribution Uplift (Article 6)

Companies fulfilling part of their ADC through equity participation provide additional NADF capitalisation beyond direct contributions. Over time, dividends and returns from citizen-owned equity holdings provide a compounding secondary income stream to the fund, analogous to the Norway Government Pension Fund Global model.

Conservative estimate of equity return contribution by Year 20: **£20–40bn annually**, rising thereafter.

### 3.3 Cost of Living Deflation

Automation does not only generate revenue — it reduces the real cost of living. As automation permeates production, logistics, and services, the purchasing power of a given UBI level increases over time.

| Category | Current Share of Spending | Projected Price Change (10–20yr) | Adjusted Share |
|----------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|----------------|
| Food | 15% | -40% | 9% |
| Housing | 30% | +10% | 33% |
| Energy | 8% | -50% | 4% |
| Transport | 10% | -60% | 4% |
| Consumer Goods | 12% | -40% | 7% |
| Services / Care | 15% | +10% | 16.5% |
| Communications | 5% | -70% | 1.5% |
| Other | 5% | 0% | 5% |

**Weighted average cost of living reduction: approximately 25–30%**

A £500/month UBI in nominal terms therefore carries the real purchasing power of approximately **£650–700/month** in today's money by Year 10–20.

**Note:** Housing represents the primary structural cost pressure not naturally deflated by automation, as land supply remains constrained. Parallel policy intervention — including automated construction programmes and land value reform — is recommended to address this. See Section 6.

## Section 4 — Government Savings from Automation

### 4.1 Public Sector Automation Savings Estimates (UK, Year 10–20)

As automation reduces the cost of delivering public services, government expenditure requirements fall — releasing funds that can either reduce taxation burden or be redirected to supplement the NADF and UBI programme.

| Area | Current Annual Cost | Automation Potential | Estimated Saving |
|------|--------------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| NHS administration | £15bn | High — scheduling, records, diagnostics support | £4–6bn |
| NHS clinical support | £60bn | Moderate — AI diagnostics, robotic surgery support | £8–12bn |
| Local Government administration | £30bn | High — planning, permits, benefits processing | £8–10bn |
| Local Government services | £50bn | Moderate — highways, waste, inspection automation | £8–12bn |
| Police / Justice administration | £8bn | High — case management, reporting, scheduling | £2–3bn |
| HMRC / tax administration | £5bn | Very High — AI-driven compliance and processing | £3–4bn |
| Education administration | £8bn | Moderate — administrative functions only | £2–3bn |
| Defence / non-frontline functions | £10bn | Moderate | £2–3bn |
| Infrastructure maintenance | £20bn | Moderate — sensor networks, automated inspection | £4–6bn |

**Estimated total public sector automation savings: £41–59bn annually by Year 10–20**

These savings do not eliminate public sector employment — frontline care, policing, teaching, and similar roles retain strong human primacy. They reflect administrative efficiency, AI-assisted decision support, and automation of routine operational functions.

### 4.2 Reallocation to NADF

A proportion of government savings attributable to automation — suggested initial rate of 50% — could be formally allocated to the NADF, creating a public sector automation dividend parallel to the private sector ADC mechanism.

**Potential public sector NADF contribution: £20–30bn annually by Year 10–20**

## Section 5 — Benefits System Rationalisation

### 5.1 Current UK Benefits Spend

The UK benefits bill is approximately **£260bn annually**. As UBI scales up, the majority of existing benefits are replaced, releasing this expenditure for redirection to the NADF or direct UBI funding.

| Benefit Type | Annual Cost | UBI Replacement | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| State Pension | £124bn | Full | UBI must meet minimum state pension equivalent (~£11,500/yr) |
| Universal Credit | £35bn | Full | Entirely replaced by UBI |
| Housing Benefit | £23bn | Partial | Full replacement requires parallel housing cost reform |
| Disability Benefits (PIP/ESA) | £28bn | Partial | Supplementary disability payment retained above UBI baseline |
| Child Benefit | £15bn | Full | Replaced by child UBI equivalent within PLEC framework |
| Tax Credits | £2bn | Full | Replaced |
| Other Benefits | £33bn | Largely full | Minor residual specialist provisions retained |
| **Total** | **£260bn** | | |

**Estimated releasable benefits spend by Year 20: ~£220bn**
**Partial release by Year 10: ~£150bn** (as UBI phases in and replaces benefits progressively)

### 5.2 Employment Support Infrastructure — Full Elimination

In a post-labour economy where human work is optional for survival, the entire infrastructure of employment support becomes redundant and can be eliminated entirely:

| Infrastructure | Current Annual Cost | PLEC Outcome |
|---------------|--------------------|--------------| 
| Jobcentre Plus network (850+ sites) | £700m | Fully eliminated |
| Universal Credit administration | £3bn | Fully eliminated |
| Employment Support Allowance administration | £500m | Fully eliminated |
| Work capability assessments | £600m | Fully eliminated |
| Work Programme / employment schemes | £400m | Fully eliminated |
| DWP staffing and overhead | £3bn | Substantially eliminated |
| **Total** | **~£8.2bn** | **~£8bn saving** |

This represents one of the most straightforward savings in the entire framework — the bureaucratic infrastructure of means-testing, job searching requirements, sanctions, and employment assessments exists solely because work is currently necessary for survival. Once UBI removes that necessity, the entire apparatus becomes obsolete.

Additionally, the **human cost** of this system — stress, sanctions, complexity, dignity erosion — is eliminated alongside the financial cost. This has measurable downstream benefits for public health expenditure.

### 5.3 Benefits Administration Savings

Beyond the employment support infrastructure, the universal nature of UBI eliminates means-testing across the entire benefits system:

| Saving | Estimate |
|--------|----------|
| Means-testing administration eliminated | £4bn |
| Fraud investigation (largely redundant) | £500m |
| Appeals and tribunal system (reduced) | £300m |
| **Total additional administration saving** | **~£4.8bn** |

### 6.3 Interpretation

By Year 10, combining all funding sources with cost of living deflation, effective real purchasing power of **~£600/month** is achievable — a meaningful contribution to dignified living, with NHS, education, and core public infrastructure retained as public services.

By Year 20, **~£1,027/month real purchasing power** represents a genuinely viable UBI, particularly as the largest remaining cost pressure — housing — is addressed through parallel policy reform.

Full UBI viability at dignified living standards is projected to be achievable in the **Year 10–15 range**, subject to:
- Automation proceeding at projected pace
- Broad adoption of PLEC across major economies
- Parallel housing reform
- Phased benefits transition managed carefully

## Section 7 — Structural Considerations and Parallel Policies

PLEC alone is not sufficient to deliver full post-labour welfare. The following parallel policy areas are recommended:

**Housing**
The single largest cost pressure not deflated by automation. Recommended parallel policies include:
- Automated construction programmes to increase housing supply
- Land value taxation reform to reduce speculative land holding
- Social housing expansion funded partly from NADF

**Disability and Vulnerability**
A supplementary support payment above the UBI baseline is retained for people with disabilities or complex needs. This ensures UBI does not inadvertently reduce support for the most vulnerable, while still eliminating the degrading assessment and sanctions apparatus of the current system.

**Transition Gap**
Phase 1 funding is partial by design. The Transitional Support Program (Article 8.3) bridges the gap between current welfare provision and full UBI capitalisation. Government maintains existing welfare floors during this period, with NADF supplementing rather than immediately replacing existing provision.

**State Pension Equivalence**
UBI must meet or exceed current state pension levels (~£11,500/year, ~£958/month) to avoid penalising retirees. The Year 20 projections comfortably achieve this. During Phase 1 and 2, state pension provision is maintained alongside partial UBI until full equivalence is reached.

**International Coordination**
Full funding projections assume broad adoption of PLEC principles across major economies. Unilateral adoption by a single jurisdiction reduces both ADC revenue and enforcement effectiveness. Regional coordination — particularly across the EU, UK, and USA — is strongly recommended to prevent regulatory arbitrage and protect smaller economies.

**Inflation Management**
Large-scale UBI introduction carries inflation risk if not carefully sequenced with automation-driven cost deflation. The phased approach in Article 12 is specifically designed to introduce UBI as deflation takes hold, rather than into an unchanged cost environment.

## Section 8 — Summary and Conclusions

The Post-Labour Economic Charter is economically viable as a phased framework. The funding picture is considerably stronger than headline UBI cost figures suggest, because:

1. **Benefits spend is not additional cost** — it is existing committed expenditure reorganised more efficiently, with UBI replacing the majority of the current system
2. **Employment support infrastructure is entirely eliminated** — saving ~£8bn annually in bureaucratic overhead alone
3. **Benefits administration savings** from eliminating means-testing add a further ~£5bn
4. **Public sector automation savings** provide a parallel dividend stream of £20–30bn annually
5. **Cost of living deflation** increases the real purchasing power of nominal UBI by 25–30% over the transition period
6. **Equity fund compounding** (Article 6) provides a growing secondary income stream over time

Full UBI viability at dignified living standards — approximately **£800–1,000/month real purchasing power** — is projected to be achievable in the **Year 10–15 range** under reasonable automation assumptions, with partial but meaningful contributions from Year 5.

The framework does not require perfection at inception. It is designed to scale with automation, becoming more generously funded precisely as automation displacement accelerates — aligning the solution with the problem it addresses.
